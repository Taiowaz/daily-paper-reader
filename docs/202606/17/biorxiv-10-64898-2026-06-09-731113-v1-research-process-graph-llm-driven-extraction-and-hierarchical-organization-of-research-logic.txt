Abstract 14 Plant biology now publishes thousands of experimental research articles each year, but their 15 core research logic, namely what questions are being asked, with what methods, and what is 16 being found, remains locked inside free text and invisible to systematic analysis. Here we 17 present a structured, 20-year atlas of The Plant Cell in which every paper is converted into a 18 typed, directed Research Process Graph (RPG) of Question (Q), Method (M) and Finding (F) 19 nodes connected by Q →M and M →F edges. A benchmarked large language model pipeline 20 applied to 2,633 Plant Cell research articles published 2005-2026 recovered >110,000 Q/M/F 21 nodes and >126,000 directed Q→M→F chains with>98% precision. A second LLM pass 22 generalises each node into a paper-independent canonical form and assigns it to one of 10 top-23 level (L1) and ~90 sub-level (L2) categories for each node type, producing the first 24 comprehensive map of plant-biology research logic at the resolution of individual research 25 questions. The atlas reveals that Plant Cell papers fall into seven canonical paper recipes with 26 characteristic Q →M→F sub-structures, that peripheral experimental techniques have largely 27 turned over while a stable methodological core persisted, and that the strongest correlate of per-28 PI citation impact is methodological breadth, not productivity or topical breadth. We release the 29 atlas as a public, browsable database with five complementary interfaces: paper views, an LLM-30 powered research assistant, expert profiles, a taxonomy browser, and a method explorer. The 31 database, available at https://rpg.connectome.tools/, turns the literature into a queryable 32 community resource. 33 34

Introduction 36 Plant biology has entered an era of genuine multi-scale productivity. The community publishes 37 thousands of experimental research articles per year, each combining genetics, biochemistry, 38 imaging, omics and physiology in increasingly intricate combinations, part of a scientific 39 literature that has expanded near-exponentially for decades (Bornmann and Mutz 2015). The 40 Plant Cell alone has published more than 6,000 research articles since its founding, and the 41 broader plant-biology corpus adds tens of thousands more. As a result, individual researchers 42 can follow only a thin slice of the literature (Landhuis 2016), and the field as a whole lacks any 43 structured, machine-readable view of what is being asked, with what methods, and what is 44 being found across its accumulated work. 45 The plant community has built powerful curated resources, including TAIR (Lamesch et 46 al. 2012), Araport (Krishnakumar et al. 2015), Phytozome (Goodstein et al. 2012), the Plant 47 Reactome (Fabregat et al. 2018) and plant ontologies (PO, TO and GO)(Ashburner et al. 2001; 48 Cooper et al. 2018),, that organise genes, pathways, traits and organisms. Sequencing 49 repositories (ENA, SRA and GEO)(Leinonen et al. 2011; Clough et al. 2024) and method 50 registries (e.g. protocols.io)(Teytelman et al. 2016) organise data and protocols. But none of 51 these capture what a paper actually did: the research (Q)uestion that motivated each 52 experiment, the (M)ethod chosen to address it, and the (F)inding it produced. This experimental 53 logic, the directed Q → M → F chain that defines each piece of work, is currently locked inside 54 free-text articles and unavailable for systematic analysis. 55 Large language models (LLMs) have matured to the point where they can read scientific 56 articles and produce structured, typed output with high precision (Lim et al. 2025). Recent 57 applications have extracted gene-function descriptions (Hu et al. 2025), materials data and 58 synthesis methods (Dagdelen et al. 2024; Polak and Morgan 2024), drug-target pairs (Luo et al. 59 2022) and disease-phenotype links (Yang et al., 2024), and entity-relation mining now spans 60 tens of millions of articles (Wei et al., 2024). Plant biology has begun to adopt the same tools: 61 PlantConnectome used large language models to mine over 71,000 plant articles into a62 knowledge graph of nearly 5 million functional relationships between genes, metabolites, tissues 63 and other entities (Lim et al., 2025), retrieval-augmented agents such as PlantGPT now answer 64 functional-genomics questions grounded in the literature (Zhang et al., 2025), and the promise 65 of combining language models with knowledge graphs to accelerate gene-function discovery 66 has been set out in recent reviews (Lam et al., 2024; Sunil et al., 2024). A parallel effort uses 67 LLM agents to conduct research end-to-end: formulating hypotheses, running experiments and 68 drafting the paper as in The AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024) and AI Scientist-v2 (Yamada et al., 69 2025). Yet all of these efforts capture entities and pairwise relations rather than the directed 70 research logic of a paper. 71 Here, we use LLMs to extract a more demanding object: the Research Process Graph 72 (RPG) for each paper, composed of typed Question (Q), Method (M), and Finding (F) nodes 73 connected by directed Q →M and M →F edges. Each RPG is a small, machine-readable map of 74 the experimental reasoning of one paper. We applied this pipeline to 2,633 Plant Cell research 75 articles spanning 2005–2026, recovering 110,295 typed Q/M/F nodes and 126,388 directed 76 Q→M→F chains at >98% precision against a manually annotated gold standard. A second LLM 77 pass then generalised and organised every node into a hierarchical Q/M/F taxonomy. The 78 resulting atlas, to our knowledge the first structured map of research logic, lets us characterise 79

An LLM pipeline extracts Question/Method/Finding knowledge graphs from 2,633 Plant 91 

Cell papers with >98% precision 92 To capture the experimental logic of a paper in a structured form, we designed a pipeline that 93 represents each article as a knowledge graph of Research Questions (Q), Methods (M) and 94 Findings (F) connected by directed Q →M and M →F edges (Figure 1A). Full-text articles were 95 filtered to retain experimental research papers (Table S1), then processed with GPT-5 via the 96 OpenAI Batch API using a structured extraction prompt, producing one JSON record of typed 97 nodes and edges per paper. A representative Q →M→F triple from a rice leaf-angle study 98 illustrates the output (Figure 1A, inset). 99 Before applying the pipeline at scale, we benchmarked its accuracy against five 100 manually annotated papers and compared three OpenAI models that span a range of capability 101 and cost: the frontier GPT-5, the lower-cost GPT-5-mini and the open-source GPT-OSS-120B. 102 All three were evaluated under an identical prompt and structured-output interface, so that any 103 differences in performance and costs can be attributed to the models themselves rather than to 104 variation in prompting or implementation. All three models extracted most nodes correctly in 105 terms of both number of nodes and edges and precision (Table S2-11), but GPT-5 performed 106 best, reaching approximately 100% precision for Q, M and F nodes while also recovering the 107 most nodes overall (95 Q, 86 M and 196 F across the five papers; Figure 1, B and C)(Table S2-108 6). The advantage of GPT-5 was clearest at the level of edges (Table S7-11), where it produced 109 at least 98% correct Q →M, M →F and full Q →M→F chains, whereas GPT-5-mini fell to 78% 110 correct for full chains (Figure 1D). We therefore selected GPT-5 for corpus-wide extraction. 111 Applying the GPT-5 pipeline to the full set of 2,633 Plant Cell papers yielded 110,235 112 nodes (28,015 Q, 25,645 M and 56,575 F). Papers contained, on average, approximately 10 Q 113 and 10 M nodes, but roughly twice as many F nodes, confirming that Findings are the most 114 numerous node type and that each question or method typically supports multiple findings 115 (Figure 1E). The same asymmetry was evident among edges: M→F edges (63,006) 116 outnumbered Q →M edges (38,174), indicating that a single method generally supports more 117 findings than a single question recruits methods (Figure 1F). Composing the two edge types 118 produced 126,805 complete Q →M→F chains, which serve as the basic units for all downstream 119 analyses. 120 The resulting per-paper graphs capture the non-linear structure of real studies. Generalising and clustering Q/M/F nodes produces a hierarchical taxonomy of plant 148 

biology research 149 The per-paper graphs use the specific language of each study, which makes the corpus difficult 150 to analyse as a whole. To uncover the categories of questions, methods and findings recurring 151 across papers, we developed a second pipeline that converts each Q/M/F text into a152 generalised form: a paraphrase that removes paper-specific entities such as gene names, 153 organisms and experimental scales while preserving the underlying scientific intent (Figure 2A). 154 Because this task is simpler than constructing the RPG itself, we used the less expensive GPT-155 5-mini with a normalisation prompt, supplying each paper's extracted list of abbreviations as 156 side input so that acronyms such as cryo-EM and MET1 were resolved before generalization. 157 Each record retains the original text, its generalised form and the modifications applied. 158 To organise the generalized nodes per type into a navigable structure, we applied a two-159 stage LLM categorisation pipeline (Figure 2B). An LLM first proposed top-level (L1) categories 160 from the generalised nodes, and then, in a second call, proposed sub-categories (L2) within 161 each L1 category. Every generalised node was then assigned to a single L1 by L2 cell, and the 162 procedure was iterated until the assignments stabilised. 163 Across the 23,456 Question nodes, this procedure produced 10 L1 categories that 164 summarise what the field asks (Figure 2C, the L1 and L2 categories per paper are found in 165 Table S1). Proteins and molecular interactions was the most studied category (17.6%), followed 166 by Gene regulation and epigenetics (15.9%), Signaling, stress and immunity (14.8%) and 167 Development and morphology (12.9%); together, the long tail of methods, computation and 168 evolution questions accounted for less than 8% of the corpus. The L2 sub-structure of the 169 largest categories recapitulated familiar research programmes. Within Proteins and molecular 170 interactions (n = 4,132), protein-protein interaction questions dominated (937 nodes), followed 171 by enzymatic activity and protein function, post-translational modifications, protein structure and 172 dynamics, and protein localisation, capturing the classic biochemistry questions (Figure 2D). 173 Gene regulation and epigenetics questions (n = 3,722) were led by developmental and tissue-174

Comparing Question-L1 and Method-L1 categories co-occurring in 201 the same paper by enrichment analysis (observed over expected co-occurrence under 202 independence) revealed strong, expected coupling along the diagonal: Metabolism and 203 transport questions paired with metabolite and chemistry methods (ratio 2.00), Evolution and 204 comparative genomics questions with genomics and sequence methods (2.18), and Cell biology 205 and localisation questions with microscopy and imaging methods (1.47)(Figure 2G). The 206 depleted cells were equally informative, marking method-question incompatibilities: Signaling, 207 stress and immunity questions were systematically under-coupled to genomics and sequence 208 methods (0.71); Cell biology and localisation questions avoided genomics and sequence 209 methods (0.73) and chromatin methods (0.69); and Metabolism and transport questions showed 210 the most asymmetric profile, strongly enriched for metabolite methods but depleted for 211 chromatin and epigenomics (0.77). 212 The same analysis applied to Question-L1 and Finding-L1 categories showed that 213 questions generally produce findings in the same domain (Metabolism and transport questions 214 to Metabolism findings, ratio 1.75; RNA biology and translation to RNA processing, 1.82; 215 Signaling, stress and immunity to Stress and signalling, 1.54), alongside some productive cross-216 talk, such as RNA biology & translation questions and Chromatin and epigenetics findings (1.64) 217 (Figure 2H). Taken together, the enriched diagonal and the 222 depleted off-diagonal define the implicit experimental boundaries of the field, mapping which 223 question types are addressed by which methods and which findings are unlikely to follow. 224

245 If questions, methods and findings couple in predictable ways, papers as a whole should occupy 246 a limited region of the possible space. To test this, we represented each paper as a 28-247 dimensional L1 composition vector of block-normalised Q-L1, M-L1, and F-L1 shares, clustered 248 the 2,558 papers using k-means (k = 7), and projected the results with UMAP (Figure 3A). The 249 papers resolved into seven well-separated clusters, which we term paper recipes, each defined 250 by a dominant Q-L1 to M-L1 to F-L1 triad: gene regulation through transcriptomics to gene 251 expression (R1), protein interactions through protein biochemistry to protein biochemistry (R2), 252 development through microscopy to developmental phenotypes (R3), signaling and stress 253 through phenotyping to stress signalling (R4), metabolism through metabolites to metabolism 254 (R5), genetics through computational methods to genetics and evolution (R6), and cell biology 255 through microscopy to cell biology (R7). 256 To resolve the experimental substructure within each recipe, we mapped the real 257 Q→M→F edges from the per-paper records to their L2 subcategories. R2 (Proteins; 497 258 papers, 13,351 chains) was anchored by protein-interaction questions answered with protein-259 protein interaction and abundance or stability assays, yielding findings dominated by protein 260 interactions and modifications (Figure 3B). R4 (Signalling and stress; 326 papers, 9,750 chains) 261 showed a strong pathogen-interaction spine, in which pathogen-related questions were 262 answered by pathogen and infection assays, running alongside a narrower environmental-stress 263 branch (Figure 3C). R1 (Gene regulation; 556 papers, 14,785 chains) contained two distinct 264 sub-flavours within a single recipe: a chromatin and epigenetics branch, in which chromatin and 265 epigenetic modification questions were addressed by epigenetic and chromatin analyses, and 266 an RNA-biology branch, in which RNA-processing questions were addressed by small-RNA and 267 RNA-processing methods (Figure 3D). R6 (Genetics and comparative genomics; 242 papers, 268 4,640 chains) consistently routed questions in phylogenetics and evolution through phylogenetic 269 and comparative methods to produce genomic variation findings, recapitulating the canonical 270 comparative genomics workflow (Figure 3E, Figure S4 for all chain networks). 271 We next asked how individual researchers distribute their work across these recipes. 272 Computing the Shannon entropy of each principal investigator's recipe distribution for the 218 273 PIs with at least three corpus papers, and stratifying by dominant recipe, showed that every 274 recipe contained both pure specialists (entropy = 0, for example Kazuki Saito in R5 and Ji ří275 Friml in R7) and broad generalists (entropy > 2, for example Nitin Uttam Kamble in R2 and 276 Chengcai Chu in R4)(Table S12); neither extreme was absent from any recipe (Figure 3F). 277

Method composition turns over, but methodological breadth per paper stays constant 287 The recipes capture the methodological identity of papers, but not how that identity has changed 288 over time. To identify which techniques have entered or left plant biology, we extracted the 289 methodological vocabulary of every paper from its M-node text using a curated dictionary of 290 approximately 305 techniques (Table S1), and compared the share of papers using each 291 technique in an early window (2005 to 2010) and a recent window (2020 to 2024) (Figure 4A). 292 The shifts were pronounced. Several techniques rose sharply: RNA-seq increased from 0.3% to 293 34% of papers, CRISPR from 0 to 21%, and ChIP-seq, IP-MS, GWAS, split-luciferase, BiFC, 294 Y1H/Y2H and small-RNA-seq each rose at least two-fold. Others declined just as steeply: PCR 295 fell from 70% to 24%, RT-PCR from 49% to 7%, Northern blot from 21% to 2%, microarray from 296 18% to 1%, with T-DNA and GUS reporter assays also dropping sharply. A core set of 297 techniques, however, remained in use throughout, each appearing in more than 25% of papers 298 across the full 20 years: Western blot, RT-qPCR, GFP and fluorescent tagging, confocal 299 microscopy and overexpression. 300 To summarise the methodological signature of each of the 17 method categories, we 301 generated per-category word clouds in which the size of each technique is proportional to the 302 number of papers using it (Figure 4B). The largest category, Molecular Biology (2,061 papers), 303 was dominated by qPCR, RT-qPCR, PCR and CRISPR; Cell Biology by GFP tagging and 304 subcellular localisation; Microscopy by confocal microscopy; Metabolomics by LC-MS, LC-305 MS/MS and metabolite profiling; and Gene Regulation by GUS reporter, luciferase assay, ChIP 306 and transactivation assays, giving each category a recognisable identity. 307 We next asked whether this turnover was accompanied by an increase in 308 methodological complexity. Counting the distinct techniques per paper across 2005 to 2024 309 revealed only a modest increase, from a median of 13 in 2005 to 2010 to 15 in 2020 to 2024 310 (mean 13.4 to 14.7, a 10% rise, trend +0.10 techniques per year), indicating that Plant Cell 311 papers have always been methodologically dense and have broadened only slightly (Figure 312 4C). The same was true at the level of method categories: papers consistently spanned roughly 313 7 of the 17 categories with little change over two decades (median 7 throughout; mean 6.7 to 314 7.1, a 6% rise, trend +0.04 categories per year) (Figure 4D). Taken together, these panels show 315 that the defining trend of the past 20 years in plant science has been compositional turnover 316 rather than expansion: each generation of papers replaced old techniques with new ones at 317 nearly a one-to-one rate, while the core toolkit and the overall breadth of methods per paper 318 remained largely stable. 319

Discussion 427 We have shown that the experimental logic of a research paper, the directed chain linking the 428 Research Question asked, the Method chosen to address it, and the Finding it produced, can be 429 extracted at scale and with high precision using large language models. Processing 2,633 Plant 430 Cell research articles spanning two decades, we recovered typed Q, M and F nodes and 431 assembled them into directed Q→M→F chains at greater than 98% precision against a432 manually annotated gold standard (Figure 1, B to D). Unlike entity-and-relation knowledge 433 graphs, which record which biological objects are mentioned together, the RPG captures why 434 each experiment was conducted and what it established, turning a paper's unstructured 435 reasoning into a machine-readable object. To our knowledge, this is the first structured map of 436 research logic for any scientific discipline. 437 Beyond cataloguing individual papers, the RPG atlas allows the field itself to be studied 438 quantitatively. We find that plant biology asks a structured rather than arbitrary set of questions, 439 that questions are tightly coupled to particular methods and findings, and that the enriched and 440 depleted cells of these couplings define the implicit experimental boundaries of the field (Figure 441 2, G and H). At the level of whole papers, this structure collapses into only seven recurring 442 research recipes (Figure 3, A to E), within which individual investigators range from pure 443 specialists to broad generalists (Figure 3F). Perhaps most strikingly, the strongest correlate of 444

16 per-paper impact is not productivity or topical breadth but the number of distinct techniques an 445 investigator (or their collaborative network) commands (Figure 3G), suggesting that 446 methodological versatility, more than output volume, accompanies influential work. We 447 emphasise that this relationship is correlational and measured across 218 investigators, and 448 that the technique count may partly reflect laboratory size, resources, and the collaborative 449 network; it should therefore be read as a hypothesis about how impactful research is conducted 450 rather than as a prescription. The atlas also revises a common intuition about methodological 451 progress: across twenty years, plant biology has turned over its peripheral techniques, replacing 452 PCR, Northern blot and microarray with RNA-seq, CRISPR and ChIP-seq, while a stable core of 453 methods persisted and the number of techniques and method categories per paper barely 454 increased (Figure 4, A to D). Plant biology has therefore re-tooled its bench without broadening 455 it. 456 As with any LLM-based extraction, the RPG atlas is accurate but not infallible, and it 457 inherits limitations that warrant attention. The pipeline can occasionally misunderstand a node 458 or attach an edge between unrelated QMF nodes. These errors could be reduced by fine-tuning 459 the models on curated examples of the expected output, and users are encouraged to follow the 460 DOI links provided in every interface to verify a node or chain against the source article. A more 461 fundamental limitation is one of scope. Our atlas rests on a single journal, thus reflects Plant 462 Cell´s editorial focus, model-organism emphasis and methodological culture. The recipes, 463 couplings and trends reported here should accordingly be read as properties of this corpus 464 rather than of plant science as a whole. The pipeline is also currently restricted to the 465 information contained in the analysed text, so questions, methods, and findings reported only in 466 figures, supplementary materials, or full-text sections beyond our input may be 467 underrepresented. 468 The central strength of the RPG framework, however, is that it is journal- and field-469 agnostic, and our future efforts will focus on testing it across the entire plant-science literature. 470 By extending the pipeline to all plant science papers, drawing on other plant journals, and by 471 incorporating full-text articles rather than abstracts alone, we aim to build a comprehensive, 472 cross-journal atlas of plant biology. Such a resource would let us ask whether research recipes 473 and question-method-finding couplings are conserved across the field or are specific to 474 individual communities, track how questions and techniques propagate between sub-fields over 475 time, and surface understudied questions and underused methods that no single journal makes 476 visible. As the corpus grows, the accompanying public database and its literature-grounded 477 Digital Professor will become an increasingly complete map of what plant biology has asked, 478 how it has answered, and where its open questions remain. We anticipate that the RPG atlas 479 will become a valuable resource for the plant science community, supporting activities ranging 480 from orienting a newcomer to an unfamiliar topic to planning the next experiment in an 481 established research programme. 482 483
