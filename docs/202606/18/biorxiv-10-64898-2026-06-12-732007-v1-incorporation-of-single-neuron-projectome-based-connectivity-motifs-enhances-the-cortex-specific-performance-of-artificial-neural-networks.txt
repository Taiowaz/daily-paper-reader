## Abstract 21 

The organizational principles of natural neural networks could inspire the new architecture design of 22 

artificial neural networks (ANNs). Analysis of single-neuron connectomes of mouse brains revealed 23 

distinct profiles of three-node connectivity motifs in various cortical areas and hippocampal formation. 24 

A connectome-informed neural network algorithm (“CINA”) was developed to incorporate natural 25 

connectivity motifs into ANN algorithms represented by recurrent neural network (RNN) and 26 

transformer-based large language model (LLM). We found that incorporation of the average profile 27 

of cortical motifs improved the RNN’s performance in noise-resistant categorization and motor 28 

learning benchmark tasks, as compared with RNNs with random connectivity. Notably, incorporating 29 

cortex-specific motifs further elevated the RNN’s performance in tasks related to the cortical function, 30 

and this effect was enhanced by artificially increasing the bias in the motif profile. Similar 31 

experimental results were verified on an LLM using Motif-Transformer for natural language question 32 

answering and brain-signal decoding tasks. Graph-theoretic analyses showed that incorporating 33 

natural motifs drove the emergence of modular and small-world properties in ANNs. Together, we 34 

demonstrated not only connectome-inspired optimization of ANN architecture but also functional 35 

significance of specific motif profiles in various cortices. 36 37

## INTRODUCTION 38 

Cognitive functions of the brain emerge from the complex connectivity among neurons. Recent single-39 

neuron connectomic analyses have begun to provide information on both long-range projections and local 40 

connections of neurons in various brain regions 1,2 . Studies of artificial neural networks (ANNs) have also 41 

shown that the efficiency of ANNs could be improved by mimicking the architecture of brain circuits. For 42 

example, local receptive fields of the visual cortex have inspired the Gabor filter design of signal processing 43 

in ANNs 3, and long-short-term memory networks 4 were developed to mimic gate-based recurrent 44 

connections of memory-attention pathways. In principle, incorporating biological circuit architectures into 45 

ANNs may enhance the capability of ANNs towards brain-like intelligence and offer new insights into the 46 

structure-function relationship of neural circuits in the brain. However, most artificial network designs have 47 

not considered the incorporation of specialized connectivity patterns of the brain, including, but not limited 48 

to, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and the Transformer that serves as the key module in large language 49 

models (LLMs). 50 

Selective connectivity among a small group of neurons, known as “motifs” 5, have been identified 51 

and characterized in various brain regions 6. For example, feedforward and feedback inhibition motifs are 52 

employed to regulate the dynamic range of input signals, which play an important role in maintaining 53 

excitation–inhibition (E–I) balance across the network, thereby improving the speed and signal-to-noise 54 

ratio of information processing 7,8 . Furthermore, the frequencies of certain connectivity motifs are 55 

significantly higher than those based on random network connectivity, suggesting specific functional roles of 56 

connectivity motifs in the brain 9. In this study, we inquired whether incorporating distinct distributions of 57 

various connectivity motifs derived from different mouse brain regions could improve the performance of 58 

RNN and Transformer in machine learning tasks. 59 

We first analyzed recently collected connectome data on the mouse brain (https://www.digital-60 

brain.cn/) for the distribution of 3-node motifs in 50 brain regions. Previous methods for determining the 61 

frequency distribution of motifs heavily depended on time-consuming sampling techniques and limited 62 

primarily to statistical analyses 10-12 . In this study, we developed a connectome-informed neural network 63 

algorithm (termed “CINA”). This method uses matrix multiplication to parallelize the calculation of 64

computable equations that are then integrated into ANN algorithms via a specially designed motif loss 66 

function. This newly added loss function serves as a structural constraint that complements the traditional 67 

error loss. While the traditional error loss drives the network toward computational accuracy, this additional 68 

loss function enforces biological plausibility in terms of network structure during the learning process. 69 

We then examined the benefits of CINA-endowed motif distributions in both RNN and Transformer 70 

algorithms for a variety of machine learning tasks, including four types of noise-resistant categorization, 71 

four types of reinforcement-based motor learning, and six standard language benchmarks. We found that the 72 

performance of RNNs was enhanced by incorporating an average motif distribution found in the frontal pole 73 

cortex (FRP) and primary motor cortex (MOp), as compared to that of RNNs with random connectivity. 74 

Moreover, incorporating FRP- and MOp-specific motif distributions led to further enhancement of the tasks 75 

related to these two cortices. Notably, such selective enhancement by cortex-specific motifs could be further 76 

elevated by artificially increasing the cortex-specific motif distribution bias. Similar conclusion could be 77 

given for motif-informed Transformer in natural language question answer and brain signal decoding tasks. 78 

Finally, we applied graph-theoretic analysis to ANNs endowed with cortical motif distributions and found 79 

that they exhibit modular and small-world network properties inherent in neural networks of the brain. 80 

Taken together, these findings demonstrated that the performance of ANNs could be enhanced by 81 

incorporating biological connectome-based motif distributions into their network architecture. 82 83

Incorporating intracortical connectivity motifs in RNN and Transformer using CINA 160 

Incorporation of connectivity architecture revealed by single-neuron connectomes into ANNs (represented 161 

by both RNN and Transformer) faces the challenge of converting key features of connectivity into equations 162 

compatible with ANN calculations. We developed an algorithm, referred to as “Connectome-Informed 163 

Neural network Algorithm” (CINA), based on the idea that recurrently connected ANNs could be 164 

constructed from 3-node connectivity motifs of various frequency distributions. Previous studies on 165 

counting motif frequency in the brain 12 and computational models 15 have used a serial sampling approach 166 

(Fig. 3a ), which is computationally demanding due to the requirement of exhaustive serial motif search in 167 

large networks. Our CINA uses an efficient matrix multiplication-based method for simultaneous calculation 168 

of motif frequencies for all 13 three-node motifs ( Fig. 3b ). For the connectivity matrix, we first defined a 169 

method to obtain the frequency statistics of different motif categories within a small network of three 170 

neurons. This is achieved by performing element-wise probabilistic multiplication of matrix entries. 171 

Subsequently, we extend this approach to arbitrary neuronal networks using mathematical matrix operations, 172 

converting the element-wise multiplication into matrix multiplication. This key breakthrough allows the 173 

incorporation of the motif distribution into both RNNs and Transformers (here we use Mamba 16 for example) 174 

based on combined use of a loss function and task error function ( Fig. 3c, d ). Both spiking and non-spiking 175

CINA-endowed motif structures enhanced RNN’s learning capability 204 

We next evaluated the performance of RNNs with CINA-endowed motif distributions in performing two 205 

categories of learning tasks. First, for noise-resistant categorization tasks, we examined the network learning 206 

of (1) visual perception using perceptual decision-making (PDM) dataset 18 , (2) static image recognition 207 

using N-MNIST dataset 19,20 , (3) dynamic video recognition using DVS-Gesture dataset 21 , and (4) spoken 208 

audio recognition using TIDIGITS dataset 22 (Fig. 4a ). Second, we examined motor learning of four 209 

benchmark tasks, using OpenAI MuJoCo 23 paradigm of the reinforcement learning for continuous robot 210 

control, including (1) balancing a single inverted pendulum (“Inverted pendulum-v2”), (2) stabilizing a 211 

chaotic inverted double pendulum (“Inverted double pendulum-v2”), (3) teaching a robot to walk (“Walker-212 

v2”), and (4) enabling an ant to run (“Ant-v2”) ( Fig. 4b ). We used CINA to endow five distinct frequency 213 

distributions of three-node motifs in ANNs as follows: (1) Averaged motif distribution obtained by 214 

averaging those found in FRP and MOp cortices (“FRP/MOp-averaged”), (2) cortex-specific motif 215

Motif-informed Transformer further enhanced LLM’s prediction capability 277 

We selected Mamba as the basic transformer block, and then modified its state-transition matrix from the 278 

original structured diagonal form to a square matrix ( 𝐀 , termed the Motif-matrix in Fig. 3d ), in order to 279 

make motif-distribution constraints applicable (named Motif-Mamba). We then used CINA to endow three 280 

distinct biologically derived motif distributions in Motif-Mamba as follows: (1) an averaged motif 281 

distribution obtained by averaging those found in FRP and MOp cortices, denoted “Average-Motif-Mamba”; 282 

(2) the cortex-specific motif distribution found in FRP, denoted “FRP-Motif-Mamba”; and (3) the cortex-283 

specific motif distribution found in MOp, denoted “MOp-Motif-Mamba”. We next evaluated the 284

Topological organization of hidden pool neurons in specific motif-endowed ANNs 339 

To understand the network properties in ANNs endowed with various motif distributions from the 340 

perspective of the graph theory, we systematically analyzed network structure of hidden pool neurons by 341 

quantifying the topological structures of post-learning connectivity weights and the distribution of their 342 

connectivity. Complex networks often naturally comprise distinct communities or modules 33 . We first 343 

examined whether embedding motif distributions into RNNs could drive the network connectivity towards 344 

modular structures similar to real-world networks. We employed the Infomap algorithm 34,35 to detect the 345 

community structures of RNNs endowed with different motif distributions, using optimal modularity as the 346 

criterion for community partitioning. The community partitions for various RNNs are shown in Fig. 6a ,347 

which displays at most top 15 strongest communities for “FRP-specific”, “MOp-specific”, and “FRP/MOp-348 

averaged” motif distributions, and each had distinct community structures. These structures were all distinct 349 

from that found for the “vanilla” RNN, which exhibits no discernible community. We calculated modularity 350 

using the directed modularity method 36 , quantified by the modularity index, and compared it against the 351 

baseline of ER random networks 37 with matched graph size and sparsity. The RNNs endowed with “FRP-352 

specific”, “MOp-specific”, and “FRP/MOp-averaged” motif distributions exhibited markedly higher 353 

modularity indices than the “vanilla” RNN, with the “MOp-specific” motif distribution yielding the highest 354 

index ( Fig. 6b ). 355

## DISCUSSION 414 

Single-neuron connectome data provide neuronal level information on the brain’s organizational principles 415 

that could help to design more efficient and capable ANN architectures. Using the available mouse 416 

intracortical connectome data, we identified the frequency distributions for all 13 possible 3-node 417 

connectivity motifs across 50 brain regions and developed a computational algorithm CINA to endow 418 

various motif distributions into the hidden-pool connectivity of ANNs. To further investigate functional 419 

consequences of such motif organization in ANNs, we selected frontal pole of PFC (FRP) and primary 420 

motor cortex (MOp) as two representative cortical areas involved in perceptual and motor functions, 421 

respectively. We found that ANNs with “FRP-enhanced” and “MOp-enhanced” motif distributions achieved 422 

the highest accuracy and efficiency in noise-resistant categorization and motor learning tasks, respectively. 423 

This is consistent with the crucial function of PFC in maintaining stable decision-making in the presence of 424 

noise interference 24 and MOp is essential for movement planning and execution 42 . Further graph theory-425 

based studies of network topography showed that ANNs endowed with FRP-specific, MOp-specific, or 426 

“FRP/MOp-averaged” motif distributions showed strong modularity and small-world features that were 427 

absent in the “vanilla” ANN with random connectivity. These results support the notion that motif-endowed 428 

network architecture underlies the elevated efficiency of ANNs. 429

Our motif analysis of the mouse connectome showed that FRP and MOp cortices contain higher 430 

proportions of non-recurrent and recurrent connectivity motifs, respectively. Such cortex-specific motif 431 

distributions are likely to serve for distinct functions of information processing. Compared with MOp, FRP-432 

enriched motif 2 provides convergent connections that contribute to information integration. In contrast, 433 

motif 13 recurrent connections were enriched in MOp, the motor area for mapping antecedent conditions 434 

(e.g., sensory cues and recent choices) onto upcoming actions 42 , where recurrent connections could promote 435 

rapid neuronal synchronization and persistent activity. These observations are in line with the previous 436 

finding that non-recurrent sparse connectivity reduces noise interference and enhances the reliability in 437 

categorization tasks 9, whereas relatively high recurrent connectivity motifs in MOp may support the 438 

exploratory flexibility required for motor learning. Such cortex-specific motif organization may emerge 439 

during evolution and development for performing specific tasks of various cortices. 440 

In this study, we found that artificially increasing the bias of motif distributions in FRP and MOp 441 

further enhanced the ANN’s performance (including both RNN and Transformer) in tasks associated with 442 

these cortical regions. These results provide additional evidence that specific connectivity motif profiles 443 

contribute to the learning of distinct computational functions. Moreover, the elevated performance of ANNs 444 

with artificially enhanced motif distributions also exemplifies biologically inspired optimization of ANN 445 

architecture for specific tasks. 446 

Taken together, our work characterized the motif organization of various brain regions, developed a 447 

computational algorithm for incorporating biological motif structures into ANNs, and demonstrated the high 448 

performance of these ANNs in noise-resistant categorization and motor learning tasks. These findings 449 

highlight the potential of connectome-inspired network design for improving artificial neural systems while 450 

providing new insights into the functional significance of specific motif profiles in various brain regions. 451 452
